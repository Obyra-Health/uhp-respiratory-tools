Initial ABG — Does the Patient Meet AHRF Criteria?

pH <7.35 and paCO₂ >6.5 kPa after initial assessment
AHRF is defined as: pO₂ <8 kPa, pH <7.35, and pCO₂ >6.5 kPa. When a blood gas shows AHRF, a 30–60 minute time-limited trial of standard medical therapy may be appropriate (unless the patient is in extremis). NIV should start within 60 minutes of the blood gas identifying the need, with a 120-minute door-to-mask target.
Important: does the clinical picture fit? AHRF is often multifactorial. Before proceeding, consider: infection, bronchospasm, sputum retention, excessive O₂, sedation, pneumothorax, PE, and left ventricular failure. The underlying cause modifies both urgency and pathway.

Background of Asthma or Pneumonia without underlying respiratory disease — Call ICU

Per UHP SOP and NIV prescription chart: patients with asthma or pneumonia without underlying COPD/NMD/OHS/CWD should not receive ward-based NIV. Call ICU immediately.
  • NIV is contraindicated in asthma (absolute, per UHP exclusion criteria)
  • Pneumonia without underlying respiratory disease — call ICU; ward-based NIV may delay intubation and worsen outcomes
  • Pneumonia in COPD patients can be managed on the respiratory ward but if pre-morbid MRC 1–3, discuss with ICU

Patient Stratification — 5-Group Framework

Based on pre-morbid state, severity, reversibility, contraindications, and patient preferences
All patients must be stratified before NIV is initiated. This decision must be made by a senior doctor (ST3 or above). The on-call medical registrar must be informed. Per UHP SOP, no patient should be transferred to Hexworthy/Honeyford on NIV unless both the NIV prescription chart and TEP have been completed.
Evidence note (2024): pH <7.25 at presentation is associated with higher NIV failure risk and increased mortality. However, recent evidence shows patients with severe acidosis (pH ≤7.25) can be successfully managed with NIV in respiratory intermediate care units — this is a risk factor for failure, not an absolute contraindication. Manage ideally in HDU/ICU but early NIV initiation should not be delayed.

Improving — continue current settings. Reassess ABG at 4 hours. Aim for semi-continuous NIV for first 24 hours. If rapid and significant improvement with normalisation of ABG, NIV may not be required ongoing — senior review required before stopping.
NIV not improving — troubleshooting:
  • Check mask fit and seal — common cause of apparent NIV failure
  • Assess patient-ventilator synchrony — are they fighting the machine?
  • Consider chest clearance — physiotherapy, mucolytic
  • Increase IPAP/ΔPinsp by 2 cmH₂O
  • Consider increasing EPAP/PEEP by 2 cmH₂O if elevated BMI
  • Ensure oxygen connected and SpO₂ 88–92%
  • If patient continues to deteriorate — urgent medical review + ICU consideration if appropriate
Weaning Criteria (after ≥24 hours):
Consider weaning after 24 hours if ALL of: pH ≥7.35 AND paCO₂ improving AND general clinical improvement. Do not stop NIV abruptly. Begin during the day — introduce longer breaks daytime first, continue overnight until completely weaned off during the day, then wean overnight. Most patients wean within 2–3 days.

Weaning failure predictors (evidence update 2024): pH <7.35 before weaning, restrictive respiratory disorder, OHS, higher PaCO₂ at enrolment, prior NIV use, longer time on NIV, poorer GCS. COPD etiology is a predictor of weaning success.

Chronic hypercapnia: Normalisation of PaCO₂ may not be achievable. Refer to Home Ventilation Team (Dr Hughes, Dr Ward, or Dr Kathiresan). Acute NIV episode may be a flag for palliative care referral.

DVLA fitness to driveDVLA “Assessing Fitness to Drive” 2023DVLA 2023 guidance: patients with OSA causing excessive daytime sleepiness must not drive until condition is controlled and sleepiness eliminated. OHS requires NIV rather than CPAP alone.
OSA and respiratory outcomesMarin JM et al. Lancet 2005;365:1046Untreated OSA independently increases cardiovascular mortality (HR 2.87), hypertension, and T2DM risk. In COPD overlap syndrome (OSA + COPD): greater hypoxaemia, hypercapnia, and higher mortality than either condition alone. CPAP treatment reduces cardiovascular events.
Combined screening rationaleChung F et al. SLEEP 2016; BTS 2019STOP-BANG and ESS measure different constructs: STOP-BANG identifies anatomical and demographic risk factors for OSA; ESS quantifies subjective sleepiness. Combined use increases diagnostic yield. High STOP-BANG + high ESS provides stronger evidence for sleep study referral than either alone. ESS also monitors treatment response — patients on CPAP/NIV should have normalised ESS (≤10).

FeNO thresholdsNICE NG80 2017; ATS 2011FeNO <25 ppb: low — eosinophilic airway inflammation unlikely. 25–40 ppb: intermediate — consider alongside clinical picture. >40 ppb: high — significant T2 inflammation. NICE NG80 recommends FeNO ≥40 ppb as supportive evidence for asthma diagnosis. ATS 2011 uses same thresholds. FeNO measured at exhaled flow 50 mL/s per ATS/ERS guidelines.
FeNO and ICS responsePrice DB et al. Lancet Respir Med 2018FeNO-guided ICS titration (n=294) reduced exacerbations by 35% vs symptom-guided therapy. FeNO >50 ppb despite ICS identifies ICS non-responders or non-adherent patients. ICS suppresses FeNO by ~30% in adherent patients — persistent elevation suggests non-adherence or steroid-resistant eosinophilia.
FeNO and biologic selectionNICE TA776 2022; Pavord ID et al. Lancet 2021FeNO ≥25 ppb combined with blood eosinophils ≥150 cells/μL identifies T2-high phenotype most likely to respond to IL-4/13 biologics (dupilumab) and anti-IL-5 (mepolizumab, benralizumab). ConfoundersDweik RA et al. ATS 2011; Taylor DR 2010ICS reduces FeNO ~30%. Smoking suppresses FeNO up to 50% (masks T2 inflammation). Atopy and allergic rhinitis independently elevate FeNO 5–10 ppb. Formal USS competency required per BTS training standards.
Drain size selectionBTS Pleural Disease Guideline 2010; Rahman NM et al. NEJM 2015Small-bore drains (8–14Fr) are as effective as large-bore (24–28Fr) for free-flowing malignant effusions and parapneumonic effusions (Rahman NEJM 2015, ISDE trial n=107). Estimated incidence <1% but carries significant mortality when severe.

GOLD gradingGOLD 2025 ReportPost-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70 confirms airflow obstruction. GOLD 1: FEV₁ ≥80%; GOLD 2: 50–79%; GOLD 3: 30–49%; GOLD 4: <30%. ABE groupingGOLD 2023/2025 ReportGOLD 2023 replaced ABCD with ABE: Group A (low symptoms, low exacerbations), Group B (high symptoms, low exacerbations), Group E (high exacerbation risk — previously C and D merged). This change reflects evidence that exacerbation risk is independent of symptom burden and that prior ABCD did not adequately separate pharmacotherapy decisions. Group drives initial inhaler selection; grade informs prognosis.
Eosinophil-guided ICSGOLD 2025; Pascoe S et al. Lancet 2015Blood eosinophil count is the strongest predictor of ICS response in COPD.
